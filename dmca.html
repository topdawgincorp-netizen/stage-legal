4. DMCA takedown notice requirements

In plain English: Your notice must include specific information.

If you are a copyright owner (or authorized to act on behalf of one), you may submit a notice containing substantially the following:

  1. Identification of the copyrighted work claimed to have been infringed, or a representative list if multiple works are covered.
  2. Identification of the material claimed to be infringing and information reasonably sufficient to permit us to locate the material (e.g., URL, user handle, timestamps).
  3. Your contact information (name, address, telephone number, email).
  4. A statement that you have a good faith belief that use of the material is not authorized by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law.
  5. A statement that the information in the notification is accurate, and under penalty of perjury, that you are authorized to act on behalf of the copyright owner.
  6. A physical or electronic signature of the copyright owner or authorized representative.

5. Counter-notification

In plain English: If your content was removed by mistake, you can respond.

If we remove or disable User Content in response to a notice, we may notify the affected User. If you believe material was removed by mistake, you may send a counter-notification containing the information required by 17 U.S.C. § 512(g). Counsel should provide the exact statutory text and process.

We may restore material after the statutory waiting period unless the copyright owner seeks a court order.

6. Repeat infringers

In plain English: Multiple infringements can lose account access.

We maintain a policy to terminate, in appropriate circumstances, repeat infringers’ access to the Services.

7. Misrepresentations

In plain English: False claims can create liability.

Under 17 U.S.C. § 512(f), any person who knowingly materially misrepresents that material is infringing (or was removed by mistake) may be liable for damages.
